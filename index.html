How I came to FAC and why I'm applying

I first heard about Founders and Coders at the homeless shelter I volunteer at on Tuesdays. When an alumnus and fellow volunteer, Haydn Appleby, found out I was coding he told me about FAC and urged me to apply. However, I hummed and hawed. I had just begun to code and I wasn’t sure I was ready or committed. He also advised me to stop learning Ruby and to start learning JavaScript, because of JavaScript's prevalence. But I was determined not to give up early, or flip-flop from language to language, and so I was, almost inevitably, going to finish my Ruby textbook no matter what.

It was only when I was at the end of my book that I looked up and considered the possibilities of my future in programming. I realised that I’d come this far and that I did enjoy programming. My quietly determined mission to get through one book had crystallised into a substantive interest in the field. By now, I was reading about transistors and ripple-adders in my spare time. I'd made a project. It was at this point that I began doing research on bootcamps.

Because of Haydn’s recommendation, FAC was one of my first stops, and it stood out for a lot of reasons. Firstly, I liked that it was in my local area. Secondly, and more importantly, I liked the ethos of FAC, its positive aims and its democratic, grassroots origins. I was also interested that FAC has links with Palestine, a place that’s mattered to me since, at 15, I did work experience at the Palestine Solidarity Campaign and, in 2016, I visited. All in all, FAC sounded like the kind of organisation I wanted to be a part of. This assessment was confirmed when I attended the pre-application coding meetups.

In fact, for my whole life, but especially since I graduated from uni (in philosophy), I’ve had serious misgivings about my future place in the job market. Unwilling to go straight into a graduate job for a corporation, I began bartending at a music venue, KOKO, to keep myself busy while I made up my mind. That was over a year ago now, and I’ve spent some serious time reflecting. I’ve begun to look at social enterprise schemes and, of course, I've begun to code.

Coding is a good choice for me for many reasons:

  • It equips me with specific, specialist skills, which I can use to do work that helps both me and others.
  • Coding requires creative problem-solving and hard graft in equal measure (like, believe it or not, a philosophy degree).
  • As a programmer, you cannot rest on your laurels. Your job will force you to continue learning, and so, hopefully, will continue to be interesting.

Adventures in Ruby

Before applying to Founders and Coders I was learning Ruby. Here are some of the things I worked on:

Alarm clock

I made this after asking a friend what programs he needed on his computer. It didn’t take me much more than an hour, but I enjoyed making it. I like how it forced me to use and abuse Ruby’s kernel features, like while-loops and the bell terminal output. The alarm clock is a bit ‘hacky’ and for some reason that makes me proud.

Web Guesser

This was an experiment in Sinatra, a Web Application Framework for Ruby, which I completed as I was finishing Learn Ruby the Hard Way and just before I expressed interest in Founders and Coders. I followed a tutorial from JumpStartLabs.

WhatsApp analyser

Wow Ivo, this could really save a relationship

I'm not sure it's going to save any relationships, but the WhatsApp analyser is the most ambitious project I've worked on. It takes a WhatsApp chat, parses it, analyses it according to 17 metrics, and presents the data in a GUI containing a table, graphs and a bar-chart. It's working, but more could be done! Here's a roadmap for where it could go:

  • Running it currently requires both CLI and GUI. I could completely transition to GUI.
  • It doesn't currently use Bundler (a dependency management gem) but does require the gem Flammarion.
  • There are more graphable metrics to be added.
  • It's currently very long, at over 500 lines. I think it's as DRY as I can make it, but I would like to separate out component parts into different files and modules
  • I could do some input control, for instance by giving the user the opportunity to re-enter the file location or skip a line if the program throws an error.

Data-types in Ruby: a lesson for the beginner

This is a short article I wrote to explain intermediate concepts like encapsulation, classes and objects to beginners via the familiar concept of a data-type. I didn't publish it because, to be perfectly honest, I'm not yet qualified to. Nevertheless I think that it's an interesting and probably-correct read.

Note that it's written from a 'Ruby' perspective that doesn't make quite so much sense from a JS one. For instance in Ruby, although people regularly say 'everything is an object', they categorically do not include methods in that statement. The JavaScript saying, by contrast, is 'functions are first-class citizens'. Because JavaScript is not a fully object-oriented language, it might be problematic to use it as an example of OOP concepts.
